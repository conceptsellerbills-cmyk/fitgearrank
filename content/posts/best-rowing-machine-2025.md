# Best Rowing Machine 2025: Air, Magnetic & Water Rowers Compared

Rowing is one of the most complete forms of exercise available. A full rowing stroke engages approximately 86% of the body's muscles — legs, core, back, and arms working together in a coordinated, low-impact movement. This combination of high caloric burn, cardiovascular demand, and total-body muscle engagement makes rowing machines one of the smartest investments in home fitness equipment.

Yet most people have never rowed properly and drastically underestimate how challenging (and rewarding) rowing can be. This guide covers the best rowing machines of 2025, along with what makes rowing such an exceptional workout and how to choose the right type of rower for your home.

## The Case for Rowing

Running burns calories but is high-impact — hard on knees, hips, and ankles. Cycling is low-impact but primarily lower-body. Rowing is both low-impact AND full-body, making it the superior choice for joint health while maintaining cardiovascular challenge.

For caloric burn: an average person rowing at moderate intensity burns around 400–600 calories per hour — comparable to running, but without the impact. At high intensity, elite rowers can sustain outputs exceeding 800 calories per hour.

The seated position also makes rowing accessible for people with certain injuries or limitations that prevent running or standing cardio exercise.

## Types of Rowing Machines

**Air Resistance:** Resistance is created by a flywheel fanned by your pull — the harder you pull, the more resistance you feel. This natural resistance curve mirrors actual rowing and is the standard for professional rowing athletes. The Concept2 is the worldwide benchmark.

**Magnetic Resistance:** Uses magnets to create resistance, which can be set to a fixed level via dial or digital control. Quieter than air rowers, more suitable for apartments. The fixed resistance doesn't automatically increase with stroke power like air resistance does.

**Water Resistance:** Uses paddles moving through a tank of water to create resistance. Provides the most authentic rowing feel and a satisfying water sound. Generally quieter than air rowers.

**Hydraulic Resistance:** The most compact and affordable type, using hydraulic cylinders. Limited range of motion and less consistent resistance feel. Budget option but not recommended for serious training.

## Proper Rowing Form

Most beginners make the mistake of rowing with only their arms and back, missing the powerful leg drive that should provide 60% of rowing power. Correct form:

1. **Catch:** Arms straight, leaning slightly forward from hips, knees bent, shins vertical
2. **Drive:** Push with legs first — extend legs while keeping arms straight
3. **Finish:** Once legs are almost straight, swing back (lean back slightly), then pull handle to lower ribcage
4. **Recovery:** Extend arms first, then hinge forward from hips, then bend knees to slide forward

A ratio of 1:2 (drive to recovery) is recommended — the recovery should be twice as slow as the drive. This rhythm is efficient and prevents energy waste.
